Introduction

\(\sigma\)-algebras as proxies for information

We consider here a sample space \(\Omega\) containing outcomes \(\omega\) of some random process. Let \(\mathcal{F}\) be a set of subsets of \(\Omega\) with the particular property:

For each \(A\in \mathcal{F}\) and each \(\omega \in \Omega\), it is possible for the observer to decide whether \(\omega\in A\) or not.

The observer’s decision is evidently based on some sort of “information” that they have, so one can use the class \(\mathcal{F}\) has a proxy for this information.

The class \(\mathcal{F}\) has a trio of very natural properties:

  1. Certainly, the observer knows that \(\omega\in \Omega\) for every outcome \(\omega\). Thus the class \(\mathcal{F}\) must contain the entire sample space \(\Omega\).

  2. If the observer can decide whether \(\omega\in A\) or not, then they can also decide whether \(\omega \in A^c\) or not. Thus \(\mathcal{F}\) must be closed under complementation, in the sense that \(A\in \mathcal{F}\) implies \(A^c\in \mathcal{F}\) as well.

  3. If \(A_1\) and \(A_2\) are both events in \(\mathcal{F}\), then the observer can decide whether \(\omega \in A_1\cup A_2\) or not, since this corresponds to deciding whether \(\omega\in A_1\) or \(\omega \in A_2\). Thus \(\mathcal{F}\) must be closed under binary unions, in the sense that \(A_1\in \mathcal{F}\) and \(A_2\in \mathcal{F}\) together imply \(A_1\cup A_2\in \mathcal{F}\).

These considerations lead us to a definition:

Definition 1 Let \(\Omega\) be a set and \(\mathcal{F}\) a set of subsets of \(\Omega\). Then \(\mathcal{F}\) is called an algebra of sets if it has the following properties:

  1. It contains \(\Omega\).

  2. It is closed under complementation:

\[ A\in \mathcal{F}\quad \Rightarrow \quad A^c\in \mathcal{F}. \]

  1. It is closed under binary unions:

\[ A_1, A_2\in \mathcal{F}\quad\Rightarrow \quad A_1 \cup A_2 \in \mathcal{F}. \]

So, we’ve demonstrated that our set \(\mathcal{F}\) of decidable events—containing our observer’s information—is naturally an algebra of sets.

Note that properties (1) and (2) together imply that \(\emptyset \in \mathcal{F}\). Since

\[ A_1\cap A_2 = (A_1^c \cup A_2^c)^c, \]

properties (2) and (3) imply that \(\mathcal{F}\) is closed under binary intersections. By induction, property (3) extends to finite unions: if

\[ A_1, A_2, \ldots, A_n \]

are all in \(\mathcal{A}\), then so is

\[ A_1 \cup A_2 \cup \cdots \cup A_n. \]

Finally, by taking complements, we conclude that \(\mathcal{F}\) is closed under finite intersections.

But what about countably infinite unions? Suppose the observer can decide membership in each of the events \(A_1, A_2, A_3, \ldots\) separately. Can they also decide whether \(\omega \in \bigcup_{k=1}^\infty A_k\)?

The answer is yes, provided the observer can carry out (and complete) a countable sequential process. To check whether \(\omega\) belongs to the union, they examine each of \(A_1, A_2, A_3, \ldots\) in turn. If \(\omega\) appears in any \(A_k\)​, they will eventually discover this fact and conclude that \(\omega \in \bigcup_{k=1}^\infty A_k\)​. If \(\omega\) belongs to none of the sets, they can verify \(\omega \notin A_k\) for each \(k\) and conclude \(\omega \notin \bigcup_{k=1}^\infty A_k\). This sequential checking procedure works precisely because the collection is countable—there is a systematic way to enumerate and verify membership in each set.

So, provided that each of \(A_1,A_2,A_3,\ldots\) is in \(\mathcal{F}\), we ask that \(\mathcal{F}\) also contains the union \(\bigcup_{k=1}^\infty A_k\). This is closure under countable unions.

The idea of “completing” an infinite process might make the reader uncomfortable at first, but it is not unfamiliar to anyone who has studied calculus. For example, when we write \(\sum_{k=1}^\infty \frac{1}{2^k} = 1\), we are asserting that an infinite process—adding infinitely many terms—has a definite, completed outcome. When we evaluate \(\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\sin x}{x} = 1\), we are making a precise claim about the behavior of a function as we carry out an infinite limiting process. In fact, the real numbers themselves are constructed via a completion process. In each case, we assign definite values to processes that would take infinitely many steps to “carry out” literally. The closure of \(\mathcal{F}\) under countable unions is in precisely the same spirit: we accept that a countable sequence of checks \(A_1, A_2, A_3, \ldots\) can be “completed” to yield a definite answer about membership in the countable union​, just as we accept that infinite series and limits can have definite values.

This leads us to a strengthening of Definition 1:

Definition 2 Let \(\Omega\) be a set and \(\mathcal{F}\) a set of subsets of \(\Omega\). Then \(\mathcal{F}\) is called a \(\sigma\)-algebra of sets if it has the following properties:

  1. It is an algebra of sets.

  2. It is closed under countable unions:

\[ A_1,A_2,A_3,\ldots \in \mathcal{F}\quad \Rightarrow \quad \bigcup_{k=1}^\infty A_k \in \mathcal{F}. \]

In this new jargon, we ask that our class \(\mathcal{F}\) of decidable events forms a \(\sigma\)-algebra of sets in the sample space \(\Omega\). (The “\(\sigma\)” is a traditional notation indicating “countable”.)

Integrals and expectations

Theorem 1 Let \(X\) be a measureable function with finite range on a measurable space \((X,\mathcal{F})\), let \(\phi:(\Omega',\mathcal{F}')\to(\Omega,\mathcal{F})\) be a measurable function from a second measurable space \((\Omega',\mathcal{F}')\) to the first, and let \(P'\) be a probability measure on \(\mathcal{F}'\). Then

\[ \int_{\Omega'} (X\circ \phi)(\omega') \ P'(d\omega') = \int_\Omega X(\omega) \ \phi_\ast P' (d\omega). \tag{1}\]

Proof. Write

\[ X = \sum_{i=1}^n x_i I_{A_i}, \]

where (as usual) the \(A_i\)’s form a partition of \(\Omega\) and the \(x_i\)’s are real numbers. For each \(i\), set \(B_i = \phi^{-1}(A_i)\), and note that \(I_{A_i} \circ \phi = I_{B_i}\) for each \(i\). Thus

\[ X\circ \phi = \sum_{i=1}^n x_i (I_{A_i} \circ \phi) = \sum_{i=1}^n x_i I_{B_i}, \]

so that, beginning with the left-hand side of Equation 1, we have

\[ \begin{align*} \int_{\Omega'} (X\circ \phi)(\omega') \ P(d\omega') &= \sum_{i=1}^n x_i P(B_i) \\ &= \sum_{i=1}^n x_i P(\phi^{-1}(A_i)) \\ &= \sum_{i=1}^n x_i \phi_\ast P(A_i) \\ &= \int_{\Omega} X(\omega) \ \phi_\ast P(d\omega). \end{align*} \]

This completes the proof. Q.E.D.

for each \(x\in \mathbb{R}\). Then

Indistinguishability, partitions, and sub-\(\sigma\)-algebras

How could our observer distinguish between two outcomes \(\omega\) and \(\omega_0\), using only the information in \(\mathcal{F}\)? If there was a decidable event \(A\in \mathcal{F}\) for which \(\omega\in A\) and \(\omega_0 \in A^c\), then our observer correctly concludes that \(\omega\) and \(\omega_0\) are distinct. But if no such event \(A\) exists, then the observer cannot decide between \(\omega = \omega_0\) and \(\omega \neq \omega_0\) by only checking membership in events in \(\mathcal{F}\).

Indistinguishability of \(\omega\) and \(\omega_0\) (relative to the information in \(\mathcal{F}\)) occurs exactly when we have

\[ \omega \in A \quad \text{if and only if} \quad \omega_0 \in A, \tag{2}\]

for every event \(A\in \mathcal{F}\). Outcomes \(\omega\) and \(\omega_0\) related in this way will be called \(\mathcal{F}\)-equivalent.

This notion of equivalence is evidently an equivalence relation in the technical sense, and thus we obtain a partition of \(\Omega\) into equivalence classes.

Theorem 2 Let the notation be as above, and write \([\omega]\) for the equivalence class of \(\omega\) under the equivalence relation (2). Then

\[ [\omega] = \bigcap_{A \ni \omega; \ A\in \mathcal{F}} A. \tag{3}\]

In words: the equivalence class \([\omega]\) is the intersection of all events \(A\) in \(\mathcal{F}\) that contain \(\omega\).

Proof. The goal is to establish the equality (3) of sets. The subset containment “\(\subset\)” is more or less trivial, so we focus on the opposite containment “\(\supset\)”. For this, suppose that \(\omega_0\) is an outcome in the intersection in (3); we need to prove that (2) holds for all \(A\in \mathcal{F}\). But if \(\omega \in A\), then \(\omega_0\in A\) as well since \(A\) appears in the intersection in (3). Conversely, suppose that \(\omega_0\in A\), but that \(\omega \in A^c\). Then, since \(A^c\in \mathcal{F}\), the complement \(A^c\) appears in the intersection in (3), and hence \(\omega_0 \in A^c\). However, this contradicts the assumption that \(\omega_0\in A\). Q.E.D.
